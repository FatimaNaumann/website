DINO

Dinosaurs were a diverse group of reptiles that dominated Earth for nearly 180 million years during the Mesozoic Era, evolving into a vast array of sizes and forms, from chicken-sized creatures to giants bigger than elephants, with adaptations like feathers, horns, and plates, and they lived on every continent, including Antarctica, thriving as both plant-eaters (herbivores) and meat-eaters (carnivores) with unique lifestyles. Their fossilized remains have been found globally, revealing diverse species with varied diets, locomotion (two or four legs), and social behaviors, proving they weren't just scaly beasts but included feathered, bird-like species.

This remarkable group included formidable predators like Tyrannosaurus rex and massive herbivores such as Triceratops, but also smaller, agile creatures, many of which possessed feathers for insulation or display, much like modern birds. They laid eggs, had upright postures with legs beneath their bodies, and some, particularly smaller feathered types, likely had higher metabolisms and were warm-blooded, suggesting active lives and herd behaviors. The term "dinosaur" means "terrible lizard," coined in 1842, but their true nature was far more complex and varied than the initial perception.

Although most non-avian dinosaurs perished in a mass extinction event 66 million years ago, one lineage survived and evolved into today's birds, making birds direct descendants of dinosaurs. Studying their fossils continues to reveal new species and insights into their evolutionary history, connecting them to modern reptiles and confirming their immense impact on Earth's history, with discoveries still being made worldwide.
